The Self as Perception
Sheikh al Mishri (ra) said in Kitab al-Jami regarding Shaikh al-Tijani (ra):
“I also asked him about the true reality of the human being: is it the spirit (rūḥ), or
the body (jism), or both together?
He replied: “It is neither the spirit nor the body.”
I said to him: “We have written, following your words, that it is the spirit, and that it
is the one addressed and intended by divine obligation.”
He (may God be pleased with him) said: “That was my view in the past—the view
of the gnostics—and that is why I had replied as you recorded. But today it has
been made clear to me otherwise: that the human being is neither spirit nor body,
but rather the perception (idrāk) generated between them when they come
together.”
I said: “If that is the case, then why is the spirit punished or rewarded?” He said:
“Affliction or bliss befalls it by God’s decree, as with other created things—not
because it disbelieved or disobeyed. For from the moment God created it, it has
been knowing, aware, remembering, and has never ceased from the remembrance
of God for even the blink of an eye. This contradicts the description of the
disbeliever which God mentioned in His Book—ignorance, heedlessness, and other
blameworthy traits. Likewise, the body constantly remembers God the Exalted and
never ceases, for His saying: ‘There is nothing but that it glorifies Him with His
praise.’ Thus, it is one of the things that glorify, and this is a praiseworthy quality
that merits only bliss and praise—so how could it be punished?”
I said to him: “Then how do we understand the reports that speak of the reward or
punishment of the spirit and the body?” He said: “That occurs to them only due to
their association with the human being—meaning that perception which arises
between them upon their union.”
Our master supported the truth of what he had said, saying: “If the human being
were the spirit, as some claim, he would necessarily know all that his spirit
contains of knowledges, secrets, and unveilings—but he does not. And it is known
that a person knows what his own innermost self contains, as is experientially
evident. Hence, it becomes clear that the human being is not identical with his
spirit. There are further proofs, but mentioning them would take us too far.”
It was said to our master (may God be pleased with him): “Perception is a
meaning, not a substance (jawhar), so how can we judge it to be a ‘thing’?” He
replied: “That is only according to the limited circle of the human intellect. But in
relation to God, all realities are equal—substances and accidents alike—as has
been reported regarding the forms of deeds in the grave and beyond, which none
can deny. Once this is established, there is no difference in addressing realities,

whether substantial or accidental.”
“So, once you understand this,” he continued, “God the Exalted is fully able to
create a perception at the conjunction of spirit and body, to name it ‘human being,’
and to address it—and there is no rational objection to this.
Then it was said to him: ‘If reason allows this, then why is that perception not
independent on its own, apart from spirit and body, since divine power is capable
of creating it separately, as you mentioned, and making it truly conscious in itself?’
He replied: ‘It has already been determined in God’s eternal knowledge that it
would not exist except when they are joined, and none can contest His crafting. He
is not questioned about what He does.’
I said: ‘If this is accepted, then to which does perception belong—spirit or body?’
He said: ‘It belongs to the spirit so long as it remains in the body, for perception is
within it; and when the spirit departs, perception departs with it, leaving the body
inert like a stone.’
I said: ‘This is with regard to the generality of creation. But as for God’s chosen
elect among His servants, perception does not leave their bodies when the spirit
departs, just as it remains with their spirits. For this reason, it is reported that they
continue worshipping God after death just as they did before it—and this applies
only to those in whom perception remains.’
Do not be misled by the Prophet’s saying (peace and blessings be upon him):
‘Whoever greets me, God returns my soul to me so that I may return his greeting.’
It does not mean that his noble body lacks perception—far from it! Rather, he is
perceiving even as in worldly life. The return of the noble spirit to the blessed body
at the moment of responding to the greeting is a distinction and an honor for the
one who greeted him. That is the benefit of the spirit’s return to the pure body.“
